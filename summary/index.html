Sometimes the purpose of taking a course becomes obfuscated over its duration; it can become hard to appreciate the plot of a story when there’s too much exposition. This narrative overview, in its brevity, serves to summarize the plot of this course and to clarify our purpose, for the sake of keeping both student and instructor focused on what’s important.

Limits & Continuity

In the beginning of the story of calculus there are some minor characters we must meet. These characters’ primary importance is to introduce us to the protagonists of calculus, differentiation and the integration, but we should know them in their own right first. The first of these minor characters is the limit.

  • Concept

    Understand what the notation \( \lim\limits_{x \to x_0} f(x) \) means.

  • Skill

    Be able to compute \( \lim\limits_{x \to x_0} f(x) \) either numerically or precisely.

The important idea behind introducing the limit is that we need language and notation for talking what what’s happening around a point \(x_0\), instead of directly at the point. While discussing the purpose of the course, we’ll gradually reveal the limit’s relationship to the derivative and how it can help us find the slope of a line tangent to a curve at a given point.

The other of minor character is the concept of continuity of a function.

  • Concept

    Understand what it means for a function to be continuous on some domain, and why many natural phenomenon morally should be modelled a continuous function.

We can only apply the techniques of calculus, define the derivative and integral of a function on some domain as a formula, if we take the hypothesis that the function is continuous on that domain.

Differentiation

Since learning the language of limits and the idea of continuity of functions, we now have all we need to define the derivative of a function and will immediately discuss two important things:

  • Concept

    Know and understand the definition of the derivative of a function, \[ \frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}x}f(x) = \lim\limits_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h}\,, \] and how you can think of this derivative as a rate of change of the function output relative to the function input, or how you can think of it geometrically as a slope of a tangent line.

  • Skill

    Be able to write a formula for the derivative of any function expressed in terms of rational functions, trigonometric functions, or power functions (e.g. square roots, etc).

The former of these is a concept you must internalize as you work through exercises, reading examples, and generally reading about calculus. (You do read, right? If not the textbook, then any helpful notes or pages online.) The latter is a skill you can practice until you feel proficient, and luckily such exercises are plentiful online. Sometimes those exercises are accompanied by solutions, but if they’re not Wolfram|Alpha can compute derivatives for you. A word of caution though: the expectation is that you can compute derivatives. Let Wolfram|Alpha be your aid, not your crutch—hopefully similar to the relationship you have with your calculator.

Once you are comfortable taking derivatives of functions \(y = f(x)\) where the effect the independent variable \(x\), the variable you’re differentiating with respect to, has on a dependent variable \(y\) is made explicit you’ll need to extend this skill, applying it to equations where the relationship between \(x\) and any independent variables is only implicit.

  • Skill

    Be able to implicitly differentiate any equation involving rational expressions, trigonometric expressions, or power terms (e.g. square roots, etc).

Just like with explicit differentiation, there are plenty of exercises and worked-out examples online, and you can compare your computations with Wolfram|Alpha’s.

In class we’ll spend time thinking about the graphs of functions, and what more we can say about those graphs given our new knowledge of derivatives. This isn’t an important skill we’re learning though. The purpose of doing this is to buff your understanding of the concept of a derivative, and give you a visual perspective on the relationship between a function and its derivative.

Beyond this, there are three important uses of differentiation we’ll talk about.

  • Application

    Be able to apply your differentiation skills to analyze dynamic situations, i.e. situations that aren’t static, that are changing over time. The ubiquitous class of problems that require this ability to solve are called related rates problems.

  • Application

    Be able to apply your differentiation skills to optimize some quantity, minimize or maximize some function, relative to some changing parameters. The ubiquitous class of problems that require this ability to solve are called optimization problems.

The first of these will require you to be adept at implicit differentiation. The second will simply be an extension of a skill that (I hope!) you’ve already practiced before in math class. Both of these will require that you be comfortable synthesizing mathematical equations and relationships from a concrete situation. And luckily both of these topics are well documented on the internet; you way want to append the phrases calculus 1 or single variable to your search phrases though.

There is one last numerical/computational application of differentiation we’ll discuss.

  • Application

    Perform Newton’s method manually, and understand that lines are very useful.

It’s more important that you understand the idea behind Newton’s method rather than be proficient at working it out manually; the goal here is familiarity, not fluency, so that one day you could implement it with a computer if you need to. If you’re practicing computer programming while taking this class though, you might as well implement it now.

In the next segment of class we’ll explore differentiation’s dual operation: integration.

Integration Basics

Now that we know basically everything about differentiation, it’s time to turn our attention to the other side of the story of calculus: integration. First we’ll surmount two conceptual hurdles, understanding what integration is and how it relates to differentiation.

  • Concept

    Understand that the definite integral \(\int_a^b f(t) \;\mathrm{d}t\) has a physical interpretation as the total amount of something that \(f\) has accumulated as its input moves from \(a\) to \(b\), but also a geometric interpretation as the signed area of the region bound by the graph of \(y=f(t)\) and the \(t\)-axis between \(a\) and \(b\). Furthermore understand what the function \(\int_0^t f\) represents.

  • Concept

    Have a working understanding of the fundamental theorem of calculus, knowing abstractly that differentiation and integration are inverse operations, and knowing concretely that \[ \newcommand{\ddt}{\frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}t}} \ddt \int\limits_a^t f = f(t) \quad\text{and}\quad \int\limits_a^b \ddt f(t) \;\mathrm{d}t = f(b) - f(a) \,. \]

Knowing these two concepts are the core to being fluent in the language of calculus. It’s important to understand what \(\int\) means if encountered outside of math class, and to be able to comfortably work with it in calculations. After internalizing these core concepts, there is a skill that the second equation of the fundamental theorem of calculus demands we practice.

  • Skill

    Be able to compute an antiderivative of a function. I.e. given a formula for a function’s derivative \(\frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}t}f\), figure out a formula for \(f\).

Unlike the analogous skill of computing derivatives, there is no reliable procedure to follow when computing antiderivatives. Instead, honing this skill involves some creativity. You will not master this skill in this class, but you’ll learn a few basic tools, and get some practice channeling that creativity. In the next course you’ll learn more tools and further hone this skill.

The first of these basic tools is simply pattern recognition. E.g. you know that the derivative of \(\tan(x)\) is \(\sec^2(x)\), so you should recognize that you know \(\int \sec^2(x) \;\mathrm{d}x\). The next of these tools is that the power-rule works in reverse: \[ \int x^n \;\mathrm{d}x = \frac{1}{n+1}x^{n+1} + C \qquad\text{for } x\neq -1\,. \] And then we’ll talk about the method of substitution, a method of undoing the chain rule. When searching the internet for integration exercises be sure to include the keyword substitution to avoid exercises you are not ready for. And when practicing, you can check your calculations with Wolfram|Alpha

Once this skill is developed we’ll talk about some applications of integration that rely heavily on wielding that skill and understanding the core concepts of integration.

  • Application

    Be able to compute the area of any region in the coordinate plane bounded by the graphs of functions.

  • Application

    Know that the volume of a solid of revolution can be thought of as the accumulation of areas of cross-sections along either an axis or a radius, and be able to compute this volume as an integral.

  • Application

    Know that work is the accumulation of force applied over the displacement of some mass, and be able to compute this work as an integral.

Each of these require the understanding of an integral as an accumulation of something. The first two are applications of integration to geometry, which feels insincere as an application, but provides good practice of your spatial reasoning. The latter one though exemplifies calculus’ utility to science as a whole.

And that’s it! That’s the beginning of the story of calculus. In the sequel to this class we’ll dive deeper into techniques of evaluating integrals, get lost in the mysteries of \(\infty\) learning to find the limits of sequences and evaluate series, and culminate the single-variable calculus adventure by learning Taylor’s theorem.
